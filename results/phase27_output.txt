              Well-supported by position (always initial/pre-root), distribution (k is most common, f least), and section enrichment patterns. But the specific meanings (k=bodily, t=celestial, f=botanical, p=discourse) are interpretive hypotheses, not proven.

  [MEDIUM     ] 'e' = demonstrative (this/that)
              e-chains are the most common feature. Collapsing them yields consistent morphology. But labeling this as 'demonstrative' rather than just 'grammatical particle' is interpretive.

  [HIGH       ] s- = verbal prefix
              Distributional evidence is strong: roots with high s- rates (h, he, ol, ch, hed) are a small, consistent subset. This is a testable structural claim and it holds across all sections.

  [MEDIUM     ] d-/o- = genitive/article prefixes
              These are the most common prefixes for most roots. Fits a noun-phrase model. But 'd-/o-' could serve other grammatical functions.

  [LOW        ] 'esed' = lion (Arabic asad)
              Based on phonetic resemblance to Arabic asad after e→a shift. Distributional evidence is mixed — esed does not cluster in zodiac where we'd expect Leo references. Could be coincidence.

  [MEDIUM     ] 'am' = water
              Consistent with Coptic/Semitic roots. Somewhat enriched in bio/pharma sections. But many roots show similar bio enrichment.

  [MEDIUM     ] 'al' = stone
              Phonetically close to Arabic al-/el- and Coptic wne (stone). Very common root — could be a function word rather than 'stone'.

  [STRUCTURAL ] Coverage = 74.2%
              This number is REAL but MISLEADING. Much of it comes from 1-2 character roots that match almost anything (e,a,h,l,d,ch). Long-root coverage is the honest metric.

  [LOW        ] Connected prose translation
              Translations are mosaics of glosses for individual morphemes. We cannot yet produce fluent English translations. Reading coherence into these glosses requires significant interpretation.

  [MEDIUM     ] Coptic substrate language
              Several roots match Coptic (h=happen, ho=face, hed=pour, he=fall). But Coptic has ~10K roots — finding a few matches among 33 claimed roots is not statistically surprising. We would need >50 matches with consistent phonetic rules to be confident.

  [MEDIUM     ] Arabic loanword layer
              esed→asad, chas→ (not a clear Arabic word), am→ma' (water, reversed). Arabic had massive influence on medieval languages, so some Arabic loanwords are expected in ANY medieval text. Not Voynich-specific.
